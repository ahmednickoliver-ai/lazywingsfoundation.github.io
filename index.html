Our approach

LLF works with local volunteer networks, health educators and pharmacies to design interventions that are low-cost, locally-sourced and focused on sustainability. We combine direct service with capacity building and monitoring to create measurable outcomes.

  • Workshops for girls & mothers
  • Reusable/disposable dignity kits
  • Pharmacy partnerships for local resupply
  • Monitoring, feedback, and continuous improvement

Detailed Program Notes & Background (comprehensive)

Background & Problem Statement

In many rural communities, adolescent girls lack access to menstrual hygiene products and appropriate education. This results in missed school days, reduced participation, and stigma. LLF was founded to address these gaps through an integrated approach: education, product access and community systems strengthening.

Context

Dogarawa and surrounding communities in Zaria, Kaduna State were selected for initial piloting during 2024–2025 due to partner readiness, community appetite and logistical feasibility. Initial baseline surveys identified key barriers: affordability, lack of private facilities, and social norms limiting open discussion about menstruation.

Approach details

LLF's delivery model focuses on:

  1. Inclusive education — sessions for girls split by age bracket, plus mother-focused groups.
  2. Local procurement — working with women-run micro-enterprises to supply reusable pads.
  3. Sustainability — pharmacy and shop partnerships to maintain availability and enable micro-earnings for local women.
  4. Monitoring — attendance registers and short satisfaction surveys immediately post distribution.
Intermediate outcomes expected

Within 6 months of a site rollout LLF expects to see:

  • Reduction in self-reported absenteeism by 30%
  • Positive knowledge gain in >70% of participants
  • Local partners engaged for continuous supply
Risk & mitigation

Risk: Community resistance due to cultural norms. Mitigation: Engage religious leaders and mothers early; provide parent sessions; recruit respected local volunteers.

Risk: Supply chain disruption. Mitigation: Multiple procurement sources and small stock reserves via pharmacies.

Operational model (logistics)

Kits are assembled by local teams and distributed through controlled registration. Each distribution includes demonstration and a short Q&A. Volunteers record attendance and basic feedback. Sites are selected with community input to ensure accessibility.

Kit types

Reusable Kit: 4 reusable fabric pads, storage bag, soap — lifetime ~1–2 years with proper care.

Disposable Kit: 8 disposable pads, soap — for contexts preferring disposables.

Volunteer roles
  • Facilitator: Leads workshop modules and Q&A
  • Logistics: Manages kit inventory and distribution
  • Monitoring assistant: Collects attendance and survey responses
  • Community mobiliser: Coordinates with local leaders and schools
